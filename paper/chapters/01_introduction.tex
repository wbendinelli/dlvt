\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Senior leaders routinely report that the years in which their formal
authority, reputation, and network---their \emph{career capital}---peaked
were also the years in which their capacity to do the work that built that
capital collapsed. The pattern is documented at scale: majorities of
surveyed executives consider exits for well-being reasons
\citep{deloitte2022wellbeing}, median CEO tenures have shortened
\citep{conference2023tenure}, and managerial burnout is now treated as an
organizational risk rather than a private misfortune
\citep{maslach2001job,veldhuis2020burnout,muller2026systematic,tu2025dancing}.
Existing accounts frame the pattern as a resource imbalance (demands exceed
resources; \citealp{demerouti2001job,bakker2017jdr}), as a stress process
\citep{hobfoll1989conservation}, or as an organizational-level capability
trap \citep{repenning2001nobody,repenning2002capability}. None answers the
question the pattern poses: why should success \emph{itself} systematically
produce the depletion, and which interventions can reverse it?

Dynamic Leadership Vitality Theory (DLVT) answers both questions with a
deliberately minimal model: two coupled ordinary differential equations.
Vitality $V(t)$ recovers toward a ceiling $\Vmax$ and is drained at a rate
that grows nonlinearly with organizational complexity $O(t)$; career capital
$C(t)$ accumulates through energy-gated impact and depreciates; and
complexity is an increasing function of the leader's own capital,
$O = O_0 + \beta C^{\eta}$. The loop closes: capital builds complexity,
complexity drains vitality, vitality gates the impact that builds capital.
We prove that this loop admits at most one interior equilibrium for all
positive parameters and that the equilibrium is globally asymptotically
stable on the open set $\{C>0\}$. At the baseline calibration---which we
present as illustrative throughout---the system settles, from essentially
every initial condition, into a low-vitality/high-status equilibrium:
status high, deployable energy chronically low. Under the power-law load
kernel the model also yields an \emph{intervention asymmetry}: equilibrium
vitality is invariant to the scope-coupling parameter $\beta$ and movable
only through recovery-side parameters.

\paragraph{Position relative to capability-trap models.}
DLVT is closest in spirit to the system-dynamics literature on capability
traps and capability erosion
\citep{repenning2002capability,repenning2002simulation,rahmandad2016capability,sterman2000business}.
Those models are organizational-level and, crucially, \emph{bistable}:
firms tip into the trap when short-run pressure crosses a threshold, and a
sufficiently large push can tip them back out. DLVT's contrast is sharp. At
the individual level, with the mechanisms modeled here, the low-vitality
equilibrium is not one basin of two---it is the unique attractor of the
positive orthant. The trap is the \emph{default destination}, not an
unfortunate branch. The difference is falsifiable, not rhetorical: the
model predicts no multistability, no hysteresis, and no tipping point
within the baseline kernel family (\S\ref{sec:propositions}), and it
reverses the managerial intuition that a one-time ``reset''---a
sabbatical, a reorganization---can durably relocate the system.

\paragraph{Micro-foundations.}
The vitality construct is grounded in the effort--recovery model of work
strain \citep{meijman1998effort} and in subjective vitality as a
measurable, fluctuating energetic resource
\citep{ryan1997subjective,nix1999revitalization,quinn2012humanenergy}.
Effort--recovery theory is the natural foundation for a dynamical model
because it is already a statement about flows: load during effort,
restoration during respite, and cumulative strain when recovery is
structurally insufficient. It also posits no depletable willpower
substance---which matters, because ego-depletion theory
\citep{baumeister1998ego} has failed meta-analytic scrutiny and multisite
preregistered replication
\citep{hagger2010ego,carter2015series,vohs2021multisite}. DLVT is built on
the foundation that survived.

\paragraph{Relation to human-capital theory.}
DLVT extends \citet{becker1993human} rather than contesting it. In Becker's
account, accumulated capital is deployed at will; in DLVT, deployment
requires vitality, and the accumulation process itself erodes the vitality
needed for deployment. The result is a wedge between capital owned and
capital usable---formalized in the energy-gated impact function---that
widens with seniority.

\paragraph{Formalism.}
Methodologically, the paper follows the tradition of small, fully analyzed
dynamical models: in ecology
\citep{may1976simple,murray2002mathematical,scheffer2001catastrophic}, in
organizational simulation \citep{pluchino2010peter}, and in computational
psychopathology \citep{cramer2016depression}. Every analytical claim below
carries a proof or proof sketch, and every numerical claim is pinned by an
automated test in the companion code, so that manuscript and implementation
cannot silently drift apart.

\paragraph{A note on terminology.}
Earlier drafts labeled the low-vitality/high-status equilibrium the
``zombie-leader equilibrium.'' We retire ``zombie'' as a theoretical
construct: it imported connotations the mathematics does not support and
invited misreading of the equilibrium as a discrete type of person rather
than a region of a continuous state space. Throughout this corrected
manuscript the object is the \emph{low-vitality equilibrium}
(Definition~\ref{def:lowvitality}); the informal label survives only in
legacy function names in the companion code (e.g., \texttt{is\_zombie}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The paper makes three contributions. First, it delivers a closed-form,
individual-level capability-trap model with a complete global analysis:
uniqueness of the interior equilibrium in the entire positive orthant
(Theorem~\ref{thm:uniqueness}), trace negativity ruling out Hopf
bifurcations everywhere in parameter space (Lemma~\ref{lem:trace}), and
global asymptotic stability on $\{C>0\}$ via a corrected trapping rectangle
(Theorem~\ref{thm:gas}); several claims of earlier drafts are explicitly
retracted and replaced (\S\ref{sec:transient},
Appendices~\ref{app:bifurcation} and \ref{app:gas}). Second, it establishes
the single-attractor contrast with the bistable capability-trap
tradition---one destination rather than two basins---as a structural,
testable prediction (\S\ref{sec:foundations}, \S\ref{sec:formulation}).
Third, it derives the intervention asymmetry with its kernel scope stated
honestly: under the power-law load kernel, equilibrium vitality is
invariant to the scope coupling $\beta$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:scope}), so
scope reduction alone cannot raise it while recovery-side parameters
can---a property of the declared kernel family, not a universal law. These
contributions culminate in six falsifiable propositions with declared
scope conditions (\S\ref{sec:propositions}) and a pre-registered field
design that could kill the theory's central prediction
(\S\ref{sec:empirical}).
